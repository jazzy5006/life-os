🍽️ Feeding

🐾 Food: Fromm Puppy Gold

4 meals per day — breakfast, two midday tethered snacks, and dinner. Breakfast and dinner are full Learn to Earn sessions — hold the meal in your hand and ask him to sit or make eye contact before each handful (5–10 min). The two midday meals are dropped as calm-behaviour rewards during his tethered awake windows: kibble goes down when he's settled or playing quietly with a toy. Nothing for jumping, mouthing, or demanding.

Each morning, measure out his full day's food (roughly ¾ to 1 cup of Fromm Puppy Gold) into his treat pouch and distribute from there throughout the day — breakfast training session, two midday tethered snacks, and dinner training session. You'll find the treat pouch and a measuring cup with his supplies. If there's kibble left over after a training session and you don't have time to keep working, just pop it into one of the two puzzle feeders or the snuffle mat — they're great for keeping him occupied and making him work for his food. Dog treats are completely fine throughout the day too — just keep in mind he needs to arrive at each meal hungry enough to eat his full portion. No table scraps please, but any dog treats are great.
Breakfast ~8 AM Tethered snack ~11 AM Tethered snack ~2 PM Dinner 8–8:30 PM
Last meal is dinner at 8–8:30 PM, during his evening awake window. Pick up his water bowl at 8:15 PM — before dinner starts. No water after 8:15 PM — this gives his bladder 30 minutes before his 8:45 PM bedtime potty and helps him sleep through the night.
🎓 Training — Breakfast & Dinner Sessions

🐾 How a Learn to Earn session works

Breakfast and dinner are full training sessions — his entire meal is earned as rewards, handful by handful. No bowl. Keep sessions to 5–10 minutes max, then anything left over goes in a puzzle feeder or the snuffle mat. He's learning that good behaviour makes food appear, which is the foundation of everything.

Short and fun is better than long and perfect. End on a win even if it means asking for something easy.

🖐️ Sit

The hand signal for sit is the OK sign — make a circle with your thumb and index finger, back of your hand facing him. Hold it in front of him and wait. The moment his bottom hits the floor, mark it with "good!" and give him a handful of kibble.

Once he's sitting reliably, try this: walk away from him a few steps. If he follows and then sits, reward the sit with extra enthusiasm — that's a bonus!

🏃 Name Recognition & Come

His name — "Timber"

Say his name once in a happy voice. If he looks at you, immediately say "good!" and reward him. That's it — that's the whole exercise.

A few important rules:

  • Say it once only. If he doesn't look at you, don't repeat it — get his attention another way first (make a funny noise, a kissy sound, say "hey!" or "puppy puppy") and once he looks at you, reward that. Then try his name again another time.
  • Don't overuse it. If he hears his name constantly, he'll start tuning it out or learn he can wait for the second call. Save it so it means something.

Come

Hold a handful of kibble in an open palm. Walk a few steps away. If he doesn't follow on his own, squat down, open your hand toward him, and invite him in — he'll come trotting over. The moment he reaches you, reward him and give lots of praise.

For now, don't say "come" yet — just let him do the behaviour and reward it. Once he's consistently trotting to you every time, you can start saying "come" as he's already on his way to you. The word only gets added once you're sure he's doing it reliably — and like his name, you only say it once.

The rule for both his name and "come" is the same: say it once, mean it, and never repeat it. If he doesn't respond, get his attention a different way rather than saying the word again.

🐾 Paw Handling

This one is less about tricks and more about getting him comfortable being touched — important for the vet and for grooming. Here's how:

  1. Ask him to sit (use the OK hand signal)
  2. Once he's sitting, gently pick up one paw with one hand while your other hand feeds him kibble continuously
  3. Feed him for as long as he lets you hold the paw without pulling away
  4. The moment he pulls away, stop feeding — no big deal, just try again
  5. Keep sessions short — a few seconds of tolerance is a win at this age
The goal isn't to hold the paw for a long time — it's to teach him that a hand near his paw means good things happen. Go at his pace.

🧩 Puzzle Feeders & Snuffle Mat

If there's kibble left over after a training session and you don't have time to keep working with him, just load it into one of the two puzzle feeders or the snuffle mat. He'll have to sniff and problem-solve to get the kibble out — it tires him out mentally just as much as training does and is a great way to use up the rest of his meal.

Mental enrichment counts as awake time. After a good puzzle feeder session, he'll be ready for his crate.

🐕 Your Dogs & Timber
Timber weighs 5.8 lbs. Large dogs can seriously injure a puppy this size even during gentle play — please keep all interactions supervised.

🐾 Since they've already met

Timber already met your dogs last week from the safety of his stroller, which was a great first introduction! Going forward, the best interactions they can have are stroller or carry-based ones where Timber is up in your arms or in the stroller — that lets everyone sniff and get comfortable without any risk of him getting knocked over.

When you're ready to let them interact more closely, please follow this approach:

  • Start with your dogs tethered or on a lead — let them greet Timber while calm and under control before giving any freedom
  • Once you're confident your dogs are calm and won't jump on him, you can let them interact more freely — but always stay present
  • If you're just walking through the house to take him outside and the dogs are loose, carry Timber — don't set him down
  • Your dogs should never have unsupervised free access to Timber. When they can reach each other, someone should always be watching
  • Never leave Timber alone with your dogs, even if they seem gentle and calm
  • If things get too rough or Timber seems overwhelmed, separate and give everyone a break
Timber may be fearless — puppies often are. That doesn't mean it's safe. He doesn't know his own size yet.

🍯 Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar) — Just Good to Know

Miniature poodles this small can occasionally have blood sugar dips, especially if meals are delayed or he gets very overtired. He's never had an episode — this is just worth being aware of.

Signs to watch for: wobbly or unsteady on his feet, glassy eyes, shivering, unusual lethargy.

If you notice any of these: rub a small amount of Karo syrup or honey on his gums, then take him to the vet. No need to call me first — just go, and let me know when you can.

Please try to keep his meals reasonably consistent — 4 times a day, roughly spaced out. You're already doing him such a huge favour by being there, and he's going to have a wonderful time with you!

🚽 Potty Protocol

🌿 Daytime — always take him outside

Every daytime potty trip is outside. Carry him to the backyard — he should only be set down on the ground on private property, not on public sidewalks or at the dog park.

  • Stand still and wait. Give him 3–5 minutes.
  • The moment he goes, say "good potty!" and give big praise and a high-value treat
  • If he doesn't go after waking from a nap, crate for 10 minutes and try again
  • Watch for signals: sniffing the floor, circling, squatting suddenly
Always take him out right after: waking from a nap, finishing a meal, and after exciting play. These are the highest-accident moments.

🌙 Overnight — litter box in his room

At night, set Timber up in the guest room with his crate door open so he can sleep inside it but move around if he needs to. Before you put him in for the night, set out his litter box with a pee pad and a small scatter of pine pellets on top — he knows exactly what it's for and the pine pellets help attract him to it.

The overnight routine:

  • Before bed (~8:45 PM): take him outside for his normal bedtime potty — praise and treat as usual
  • Set up the litter box in his room before closing the door for the night
  • Overnight: he may use the litter box, he may sleep through — either is totally fine
  • Morning (~8 AM): as soon as you open the door, carry him straight outside for a proper morning potty — praise and treat
  • Collect and clean the litter box as part of the morning routine
Outside potty trips are what gets celebrated — big praise, treats, the works. The litter box is just a quiet safety net for overnight. No need to reward it, and no need to stress about it either way.
If there's an accident outside the litter box, use the enzymatic cleaner in the laundry room closet — regular cleaner leaves a scent that draws him back to the same spot.
🏠 Crate & Naps

🏠 Crate basics

I'll set up a travel crate at your place before I leave — it's his safe space and he's very used to it. Never use it as punishment. At 12 weeks he still needs a lot of sleep. If he seems wired or bitey, he's almost certainly overtired and needs a nap, not more play.

When he whines in the crate, wait for a 10-second pause before letting him out. Letting him out mid-whine teaches him that whining = freedom.
The 10-minute rule only applies after waking up: if he doesn't potty after a nap, crate him for 10 minutes and try again. If you're putting him in for a nap and he doesn't go before — that's fine, just put him in. No need to wait.
